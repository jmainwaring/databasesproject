Main findings

This page explains the main takeaway from our research. We set out to understand if re-assigning polling places has an impact on voter turnout, focused on North Carolina in 2016. Please note the following graph:


Effect on turnout


The chart above shows the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) of getting a new polling place on voter turnout at various distance thresholds (in miles). If the point estimate is above 0, this means individuals who were reassigned polling place were more likely to vote, whereas a point estimate below 0 means the opposite. The bars represent 95% confidence intervals - therefore, if the bottom of the confidence interval is still above 0, we are fairly confident that there is a significant effect of having one's polling place reassigned.

As we can see, there appears to be a positive treatment effect - those with new polling place assignments are in fact more likely to vote.


Significance

The findings were initially surprising. Going into the study, we believed that people whose polling place changed would have lower turnout, since they had the additional mental burden of finding their new polling place. However, particularly for the demographic we needed for our study - older individuals who have not moved in recent years - some research suggests that new polling place assignment mailers can serve as an unofficial reminder to go out and vote, which we know has a positive impact on turnout.
